Background

Albert Michelson (1852–1931) was an American physicist known for his work on measuring the speed of light. He was the first American to win the Nobel Prize for science (1907). The Michelson dataset from the HistData package contains experimental measurements of the velocity of light in air, which Michelson gathered from June 5 to July 2, 1879 and reported in Michelson (1882). The reported data values plus 299,000 are Michelson’s actual experimental measurements in km/sec—he subtracted 299,000 to make his subsequent calculations easier to do by hand. To compensate for refraction due to air temperature when he estimated the speed of light in air, Michelson added 12 km/sec to the data. When further estimating for the speed of light in a vacuum, he added an additional refraction correction of 80 km/sec. Variability in the data observations is assumed to be caused by the experimental equipment and method, so the mean of the measurements is used to estimate the velocity.

Currently accepted values for the velocity of light are 299,792.458 km/sec in a vacuum and 299,702.547 km/sec in air. The ratio of these two values is 1.0003, or three ten-thousandths of the velocity of light in a vacuum.

Reportedly, when asked why he chose to study light, Michelson once said, “because it’s so much fun.”

You will estimate the velocity of light from Michelson’s experimental data, using interval estimates not yet developed in his time.

Create new variables in the dataset to represent Michelson’s corrections to his data. Then glimpse() the revised dataset.

  • vel_air = add 12 to the observed velocity values to get the temperature-corrected velocity in air
  • vel_vac = add 80 to the corrected velocity in air to get the refraction-corrected velocity in a vacuum
newLightObv <- lightObv %>%
mutate(vel_air = 12 + velocity,
       vel_vac = 80 + vel_air)
